Person-First Language Cheat-Sheet

A free one-pager from Edward at Caira. The short, practical version — for your notes and your everyday work.

The golden rule: ask the person how they describe themselves. These are general conventions for Australian disability support; individual preference and identity-first choices always win. This is guidance, not a rulebook.

Say this → not that

Lead with the personInstead of
a person with disabilitya disabled person / the disabled
uses a wheelchairwheelchair-bound / confined to a wheelchair
a person with autism / an autistic person (ask their preference)suffers from autism
has an intellectual disabilityintellectually disabled / "low-functioning"
communicates using…non-verbal (as a label)
receives support with…dependent on / a burden
a person without disabilitynormal / able-bodied (as the contrast)

Principles behind it

  • Person first, condition second. The person isn't their diagnosis.
  • Describe the support, not the deficit. "needs support with meals", not "can't feed themselves".
  • Neutral, not pity or praise. Avoid "suffers from", "brave", "inspiring" by default.
  • Observable, not assumed. In notes, write what you saw — not what you guessed they felt.
  • Their words win. Identity-first ("autistic person", "Deaf") is many people's preference. Ask.

In your notes specifically

  • Factual and respectful — about the person, not the label.
  • "Participant declined the activity" ✓ — not "was uncooperative" ✗
  • Record support and response, not character judgements.
