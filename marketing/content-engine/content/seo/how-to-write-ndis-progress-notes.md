A progress note records what happened during a support shift and how it relates
to the participant's goals. Good notes protect the participant, the worker and the
provider — and they don't have to take an hour. Here's how to write them well.

## The 5 bones of a good progress note

1. **When** — date, and start/finish time of the support.
2. **Who** — the participant, and anyone else present.
3. **Goal** — which of the participant's goals this shift worked toward.
4. **What happened** — what you actually did and what the participant did.
5. **Response** — how the participant responded, and anything that changed.

If a note has these five, it tells a clear story to anyone who reads it later.

## Write facts, not opinions

State what you observed, not your interpretation dressed up as fact.

- ✅ "Participant declined lunch and said they weren't hungry."
- ❌ "Participant was being difficult about lunch."
- ✅ "Used the handrail and walked to the letterbox unaided."
- ❌ "Doing much better, seems happier."

If you must record a concern, write the observation that led to it.

## Use person-first, respectful language

Notes can be read by the participant and others. Keep them respectful and about
the person, not a label (e.g. "a person with disability", "uses a wheelchair").
Ask the person how they prefer to be described.

## Link the note to goals

A note that connects to a goal shows *progress*, not just activity. Instead of
"went to the shops", write which goal it supported (e.g. building independence
with money), what you did, and how it went.

## Incident notes

If something goes wrong, keep it calm and factual: what happened, when, who was
involved, what you did, and who you told. Stick to observable facts, write it as
soon as it's safe to, and follow your provider's reporting timeframes.

## The habit that makes it painless

Capture the note **in the moment** — a voice memo or three bullets during/right
after the shift — then tidy it later. Five minutes while it's fresh beats an hour
from memory at home. Tools like [Caira](https://caira.net.au) are built to make

## Self-check

> If someone read this note in two years with zero context, would they understand
> what happened, why, and how the participant responded?

If yes, you're done.

## FAQ

**What should an NDIS progress note include?**
When, who was present, the goal it supported, what happened, and the participant's
response — written in plain, factual language.

**How long should a progress note be?**
Long enough to tell the story clearly and no longer. A few precise sentences often
beats a page.

**Can I write notes the next day?**
You can, but capturing key points in the moment makes them faster and more
accurate. Follow your provider's policy on timing.

**Do my notes have to be a certain format to be compliant?**
Follow your provider's policies and current NDIS requirements. No template or app
can guarantee a compliance outcome — clear, factual, goal-linked notes are the goal.
