## Where paper and spreadsheets are genuinely fine

- You've got one or two participants and plenty of time.
- You're just starting and testing whether this is for you.
- You have a system you trust and it isn't costing you evenings.

No shame in low-tech. The question is only whether it's costing you time, sleep,
or confidence in your records.

## Where they start to hurt

- **Notes from memory.** Paper-at-home means reconstructing the shift hours later — slower and less accurate.
- **No structure.** It's easy to leave out the bones a note needs (when, who, goal, what happened, response).
- **Hard to find anything.** Six months on, good luck searching a notebook.
- **Privacy risk.** A notebook in a car or a spreadsheet in the wrong folder is a real risk with sensitive participant information (Privacy Act 1988).

## FAQ

**Is it OK to keep NDIS notes on paper?**
Yes, if they're clear, factual, kept securely and meet your provider's
requirements. The practical issues are speed, legibility, searchability and
privacy — which is where digital tools help.

**Can I just use a spreadsheet or my phone's notes app?**
Absolutely, especially early on. Capturing in the moment beats writing from memory
either way. A purpose-built tool adds structure, organisation and data handling
you'd otherwise do by hand.

**Will switching to an app make my notes compliant?**
No tool can guarantee compliance — that comes from your practice and your
provider's policies. A good tool *helps* you write clearer, more consistent notes.
